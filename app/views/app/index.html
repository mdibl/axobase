Laboratory Axolotl

The laboratory axolotl is a hybrid salamander that carries genes of natural axolotl (Ambystoma mexicanum) and tiger salamander (Ambystoma tigrinum). The laboratory axolotl is a common research model for tissue regeneration studies due to its tremendous ability to regenerate many different body parts.

Additionally, they are commonly used for developmental studies due to ex vivo development of their eggs. The first laboratory axolotls were imported from Mexico to Paris in 1860s and given to Jardin des Plantes. Most of the axolotls raised in laboratories across the world today, are descendants of those animals.

In nature, the axolotls are found in lake Xochimilco of Mexico. In nature, the axolotls are critically endangered species, and the wildtypes are black pigmented animals. Both natural and laboratory axolotl spend their lives in neoteny and maintain their gills and tailfin throughout their lifespan. Upon exposure to thyroid or iodine, neotenic animals can transform into metamorphic animals.
